Our evals lied to us.

We were building an AI support assistant for a fintech team.
It answered customer questions and suggested next steps.
In staging, it looked solid.
In production, it gave 1 wrong answer out of 20, and that one answer was about money.

We had used an eval set, which is a small test pack of example questions, to grade the model before launch.
The score was 94%.

So we pushed.

The problem was the eval set.
It was full of clean, obvious questions from our team.
Real users wrote messy things like “why did I get charged twice?” and “I already refunded this, now what?”

Those cases needed context from two systems, not one.
Our tool schema, the menu of actions the bot can take, only covered the billing app.
It never checked the ledger.

We rebuilt the evals around real support tickets.
We added 312 cases from Zendesk, the customer support tool.
The score dropped to 71%.

That was the first honest number we had.

The lesson: if your evals don’t look like reality, they are not quality control.
They are theater.
