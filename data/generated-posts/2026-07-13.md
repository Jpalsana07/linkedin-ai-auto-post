Zod saved our forms.

TypeScript was supposed to catch bad data, but the bugs still slipped through.
We were building a checkout flow, and one wrong email or empty amount could block real money.

I compared Zod and Yup.

Both validate user input.
Both let you define rules like “this field is required” or “this number must be positive.”

But Zod gave us something TypeScript could actually use.
Its schemas infer types automatically, which meant fewer hand-written interfaces and fewer places for drift.

With Yup, we kept writing the shape twice.
That extra duplication cost us about 2 days on the first form, then another bug showed up when the frontend and backend disagreed on a field name.

After switching to Zod, we removed around 140 lines of duplicate type code and cut validation bugs to zero for that flow.

My lesson: when the problem is “the code and the rules keep drifting apart,” pick the tool that makes one source of truth unavoidable.
