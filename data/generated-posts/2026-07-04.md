The checkout app crashed Friday.

We were building a payment flow for a small e-commerce client.
Their customers were stuck at the final step, and every failed checkout meant lost orders.

The frontend looked fine.
The API looked fine.
But every few minutes, the whole flow would hang for 30 seconds and then fail.

We blamed Stripe first.
Then the database.
Then the network.

The root cause was boring: one service was doing too much.
The monolith — one big app with shared responsibilities — was handling login, cart updates, payment validation, and receipt emails in the same request path.

A slow email call was blocking the payment response.

We moved receipt sending to a queue — a background line for delayed work — and split payment validation into its own service.
The next deploy cut checkout latency from 8.4 seconds to 1.1 seconds.

What I learned: when one user action depends on too many jobs finishing at once, the whole product feels broken.

Simple architecture is not about being clever.
It is about making the critical path — the steps that must finish now — as short as possible.

Which part of a product do you think is most often doing too much work?
