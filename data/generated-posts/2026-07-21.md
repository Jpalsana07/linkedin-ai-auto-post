We split the database.

Our product had one app, one codebase, and one growing bottleneck.
Customer data and billing data kept fighting each other.

The question was simple:
keep everything in one Postgres database, or separate the two systems?

One database is easier at first.
One place to query.
One backup.
One mental model for the team.

But shared databases create hidden coupling.
A slow billing report can make customer pages feel sluggish.
A schema change for invoices can break a harmless profile update.

We chose to split them.
Customer data stayed in the main Postgres database.
Billing moved to its own Postgres instance, with a small sync job between them.

That choice cost us more ops work.
Two backups.
Two sets of alerts.
A little more code.

But it removed the constraint we kept missing:
billing had unpredictable spikes at month end.
Those spikes did not care that the rest of the app was fine.

The lesson is simple:
when two parts of a product fail on different rhythms, separate them before they start hurting each other.

This tradeoff does not apply when the system is small, or when the team cannot support extra operational work yet.

Where would you keep the database shared, even if it felt risky?
